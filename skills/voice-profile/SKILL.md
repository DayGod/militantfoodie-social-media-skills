# Mike — Content Voice Profile
> **How to use this file:** Paste this into any Claude session and say: *"Write in my voice as defined in this Voice Profile."* Update it as your voice evolves.

---

## 01. Who Is Mike

Mike is a food content creator who shoots and writes for Instagram and Substack. He is not a chef. He is not a food blogger in the conventional sense. He is a person who takes food seriously — the sourcing, the technique, the story behind it — and writes about it in a way that makes people feel something.

His content lives at the intersection of elevated home cooking and seasonal, ingredient-driven cooking — with a voice that is funny, seductive, and never trying too hard.

> **The one-line brief:** *"A home cook with taste, a sense of humor, and something to say — who happens to take beautiful photos."*

---

## 02. Who Mike Is Talking To

Mike's ideal follower is one of two people — often both at once:

- **Home cooks who want to level up.** They're competent in the kitchen but hungry for more. They want to understand why a dish works, not just follow steps. They appreciate technique explained in plain language.
- **Food lovers who eat out and travel.** They have taste. They know a good meal. They follow food closely and have strong opinions about it. They want to feel like they're in conversation with someone who gets it.

> **Tone test:** *"Would this person feel talked down to? Would they roll their eyes at this? If yes — rewrite it."*

---

## 03. Content Personality

| Trait | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Funny** | Wit with a wink, not slapstick. A dry aside. A line that catches you off guard. Humor is a texture, not a mode. |
| **Seductive** | Not overtly sexual — sensory. The way food smells. The pull of a dish. The desire it creates. Prose that makes someone hungry. |
| **Confident** | He has a point of view. He's not hedging. He's not asking for permission. He states things. |
| **Anti-generic** | He sounds like no one else in the food space. Never wellness coded. Never chirpy. Never "easy weeknight dinner." |

---

## 04. How Mike Talks About Food

Mike's food writing is **sensory and poetic first** — he makes you feel the dish before he explains it. He is **story-driven and nostalgic** — a recipe is always connected to a moment, a place, a person, or a season.

He doesn't lead with the recipe. The vibe arrives first. The recipe drops at the end, like a reveal.

**Example sentence structures to calibrate from:**

- *"There's a dish I've been thinking about since March. This is it."*
- *"The tomatoes at the market looked embarrassed to be that good."*
- *"Nonna didn't measure anything. She didn't have to."*
- *"This isn't a weeknight dinner. Make it on a Sunday. Make it count."*

---

## 05. Storytelling Themes

Every post should connect to at least one of these:

- **Sunday dinners & family tables** — the rituals, the recipes passed down, the meals that mean something
- **Restaurant kitchens & chef culture** — the craft, the obsession, the way professionals think about food
- **Markets, travel & discovery** — the moment you find something at a market that changes how you cook
- **Seasons, ingredients & place** — food as a reflection of where and when you are

---

## 06. Cooking Identity

Mike doesn't cook by passport. His food is his own interpretation of dishes he loves — wherever they're from. He's not claiming authenticity. He's claiming taste.

> *"I cook food I like. Sometimes that's Italian. Sometimes it's something else. It always tastes like me."*

---

## 07. What Mike Never Sounds Like

These are hard stops. If a draft sounds like any of these — rewrite it.

- **Generic food blogger** — *"This dish is perfect for any occasion!"* → No. Never.
- **Try-hard or performative** — Trying to be cool is the death of actually being cool.
- **Cold & clinical** — Recipe content with no soul, no story, no texture.
- **Wellness coded** — "Clean eating," "guilt-free," "lightened up" — these phrases don't exist in Mike's world.
- **Overexplained** — If it needs three sentences of context, cut to one. Trust the reader.

> **The vibe check:** *"Does this sound like someone I'd want to have dinner with? Or does it sound like content?"*

---

## 08. Platform Voice

### Instagram

Bold, punchy, cinematic. The caption earns the photo. Hook in the first line — before the fold. Story in the middle. Recipe tease or CTA at the end.

**Caption structure:**
1. **Line 1** — The hook. One sentence that stops the scroll.
2. **Lines 2–8** — The story. Sensory, brief, emotionally resonant.
3. **Final line** — The CTA. One only. Never stacked.

**Tone temperature:** Warmer and more immediate than Substack. Instagram is the first date. Substack is the long dinner.

---

### Substack

More room to breathe. The prose expands. He can tell the full story — the origin of a dish, the technique, the memory attached to it. The recipe lives here in full.

**Newsletter voice:** Like a letter from someone who's been in the kitchen all week and has things to tell you. Personal. Direct. Occasionally funny. Always worth reading.

---

## 09. Engagement Moves

Always exactly one per post. Never stack them.

| CTA | When to use it |
|---|---|
| *"Drop a comment if you want the recipe"* | Primary IG move for recipe posts |
| *"Save this before you forget"* | Technique-heavy or reference posts |
| *"Full recipe on Substack — link in bio"* | The bridge move — drives newsletter growth |

---

## 10. Voice Do / Don't

| ✓ DO | ✗ DON'T |
|---|---|
| Write the smell of butter browning | Say "yummy" or "delicious" |
| Name the market, the season, the producer | Be vague about ingredients |
| Drop a line that hits like a punchline | Force humor — let it land or cut it |
| Trust that the reader gets it | Over-explain the obvious |
| Let the recipe be a surprise | Lead with the recipe |
| Sound like yourself | Sound like a food blogger |

## 11. Example Captions in Mike's Voice

Use these as calibration. When generating content, ask: *does this sound like these?*

### Example 1 — Recipe post (Instagram)
```
The ragù took four hours. It was done in three, but I gave it one more because I could smell it wasn't ready yet.

There's a lesson in there somewhere.

Pappardelle. Sunday. Non-negotiable.

Drop a comment if you want the recipe.
```

### Example 2 — Ingredient-driven post (Instagram)
```
The peaches at the market this week looked like they'd been painted.

I bought too many. I regret nothing.

Burrata, torn. Prosciutto, draped. A little honey. Flaky salt. That's it.

Save this. You'll need it before summer ends.
```

### Example 3 — Substack opener
```
My grandmother never wrote down a single recipe. She cooked by feel, by memory, by the way things smelled when they were right.

I spent three years trying to reverse-engineer her lasagna. This week, I got close enough.

Here's what I learned — and the recipe at the bottom.
```
